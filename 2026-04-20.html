The Coordination Tax

How AI is deleting the hidden overhead that defines most of knowledge work — while the infrastructure to support it fractures under geopolitical strain

There's a thesis gaining traction this week that reframes the entire conversation about AI and jobs. It isn't about automation. It isn't about which tasks a model can perform. It's about something more foundational: most of what knowledge workers do all day isn't value creation — it's coordination overhead. PRDs, sprint planners, status updates, alignment sessions, design handoffs, onboarding docs. These exist not because they produce value directly, but because the execution layer is made of humans with limited bandwidth. When agentic systems can go from insight to shipped code in one loop, they don't automate your tasks. They make the reason for those tasks disappear entirely.

Meanwhile, the hardware that AI runs on faces a structural crisis. A bromine shortage triggered by Middle East conflict is threatening global memory chip production. RAM demand won't be fully met until at least 2028. And this week's biggest security stories — a major breach at Vercel, an email-leaking bug at Notion, and Anthropic quietly installing native messaging bridges in Chrome — are a reminder that the infrastructure transition to an AI-first world is happening faster than our security and supply chains can absorb it.

At the same time, Demis Hassabis sat down with Google DeepMind's own podcast to describe, in unusually candid terms, exactly what's missing from today's frontier models before we reach AGI. The answer isn't more parameters. It's something stranger and harder.

The Coordination Collapse

Jack Dorsey laid off 4,000 people at Block and blamed AI. This week, a pair of NateBJones essays dug into what's actually happening — and the conclusion is more disorienting than "AI took the jobs." The jobs, it turns out, were never quite what we thought they were.

Consider the typical knowledge worker's week. Microsoft's Work Trend Index found that the average employee spends 57% of their time communicating and only 43% creating. Separately, Sana's Anatomy of Work put the number at 60% "work about work." The average knowledge worker sits through 11.3 hours of meetings per week, a number that has tripled since 2020. That's not a productivity failure — it's the organizational mechanic required to coordinate execution through a layer made of humans who can't share context instantly, can't see each other's progress in real time, and need translation artifacts (the PRD, the spec, the deck, the ticket) to bridge between people who weren't in the same room.

"We don't experience these activities as what they really are — as overhead. We experience them as the role. But the value is working software that solves a customer problem. That's it. Everything between 'we understand what to build' and 'the thing exists and works' is process."

The insight that cuts deeper: this coordination tax isn't automated away by AI. It's structurally eliminated. An agent harness starting a new task reads the codebase, reads the progress file, reads the git history. Full context in seconds. The onboarding problem — transferring institutional knowledge from human brains to a new human brain — simply doesn't exist for agents. Neither does the synchronization problem between people who can't share a brain. The handoffs don't get automated. They don't happen.

The revenue-per-employee numbers at AI-native companies make this concrete. While legacy SaaS benchmarks hover below $500K per employee, AI-native companies — Lovable, Midjourney, ElevenLabs, Anthropic, OpenAI — are running five to ten times that. At a $2–3M per-employee output baseline, the coordination cost of adding a sixth person to a five-person team isn't a minor tax. It's catastrophic. The math that made large organizations economical no longer holds.

Robin Dunbar established in 1992 that the human brain can sustain deep, high-context coordination with about five people. Army fire teams, Jeff Bezos's two-pizza rule, Fred Brooks's software engineering findings — all converged on the same number from different disciplines. What AI changes isn't that number. It's what happens when you violate it: the cost penalty jumped from manageable to devastating. Your meetings aren't a meetings problem. They're a team size problem.

Demis Hassabis and the Missing Pieces

In a wide-ranging end-of-year conversation on the Google DeepMind podcast, Demis Hassabis laid out his clearest public map yet of what separates today's frontier models from AGI. He was unusually direct about what's broken.

"Jagged intelligence" was his phrase. Today's systems perform at PhD level in certain dimensions — winning gold at the International Math Olympiad — and below high school level in others. They struggle with basic chess moves. They can't reliably count letters in words. The gap isn't about raw intelligence; it's about consistency across the board, which is precisely what you'd expect from a genuinely general intelligence.

Hassabis identified three structural missing pieces. First, consistency in reasoning — the models need to do what humans do on hard questions: pause, check their own output, and use tools to verify before committing to an answer. "These models are talking like a person on a bad day — just saying the first thing that comes to their mind. Sometimes that's fine. On hard problems, you need to stop and double-check." Inference-time thinking helps, but "we're only 50% of the way there."

"Nobody's found anything in the universe that's non-computable. So far."

Second, online learning. Today's models are trained, balanced, post-trained, and deployed — and then they stop learning. They don't continue to update from what they encounter in the world, the way humans do. Hassabis called this a "critical missing piece." Third: world models. Language is richer than linguists expected, and LLMs learned more about the world from text than many thought possible. But the spatial, physical, causal dynamics of the world — how things move, how things interact — can't be fully described in language. Genie, DeepMind's interactive world model, is one path toward filling that gap. Simmer, their agent that operates inside Genie's generated worlds, shows what the training loop looks like when it closes: the agent explores, Genie generates the world around it, and the two systems pull each other toward harder and harder tasks automatically.

On scaling, Hassabis pushed back on the wall narrative. "There's a lot of room between exponential and asymptotic." Google's approach is 50% scaling, 50% research innovation. The bet: when the terrain gets harder, you need both world-class engineering and world-class science. "I actually like it when it gets harder," he said. "That's when research-first organizations have an advantage."

Supply Chain Under Siege: The Bromine Chokepoint and the RAM Drought

While AI labs race to build AGI, the hardware that AI runs on is facing a confluence of structural crises that could constrain the buildout for years.

First: RAM. The Verge reported this week that memory manufacturers are expected to meet only 60% of demand by the end of 2027. The shortage isn't a blip — it's a multi-year structural imbalance between AI's voracious appetite for memory bandwidth and the physical pace at which new fabrication capacity can be brought online. AI training runs and inference clusters consume memory at a scale that wasn't designed for when today's fabs were built. The shortfall compounds: underbuilt now means constrained through 2028 at the earliest.

Second, and less discussed: bromine. The US-Israeli war with Iran, now in a brittle ceasefire, has exposed a hidden vulnerability in the global semiconductor memory supply chain. Bromine — a relatively obscure chemical element — is a critical ingredient in the flame retardants used in memory chip manufacturing. The Dead Sea basin, sitting squarely in the conflict zone, produces a disproportionate share of the world's bromine supply. A War on the Rocks analysis this week laid out the math: if conflict disrupts bromine production significantly enough, memory chip manufacturing could be throttled at a structural level, with no easy substitute path.

The confluence is uncomfortable. AI's infrastructure ambitions — more training clusters, more inference capacity, more memory bandwidth — are running directly into geopolitical and material constraints that have nothing to do with model architecture. The bottlenecks aren't intellectual. They're chemical and physical.

Security Breaks on All Fronts

The biggest story on Hacker News today: Vercel confirmed a security incident after threat actors claimed to have breached its systems and are attempting to sell stolen data. With 584 points and 331 comments at the time of writing, this is the most-discussed story of the day. Vercel is one of the primary deployment platforms for modern web applications — any breach has downstream implications for the teams and products hosted on it.

Two other security stories deserve attention. A security researcher discovered that every public Notion page leaks the email addresses, full names, and profile photos of every editor, with zero authentication required — one POST request, no cookies, no tokens. If your company wiki is public, every employee's email is exposed. Notion had not commented at time of writing, and the bug has 335 points on HN.

More nuanced: a privacy researcher documented that Anthropic's Claude Desktop silently installs a Native Messaging bridge into seven Chromium-based browsers, including browsers that Anthropic's own documentation lists as unsupported, and browsers the user hasn't installed. The researcher called it spyware; the more accurate framing is probably "undisclosed side effect of Claude's browser integration feature." But it surfaces a real question: as AI products integrate deeper into local systems, the permissions model for what they install, and where, needs to be much more transparent than it currently is.

The Claude story connects to another interesting HN thread from the weekend: Simon Willison's analysis of what changed between Claude Opus 4.6 and 4.7's public system prompts. Anthropic is the only major AI lab that publishes the system prompts for its user-facing chat products. Willison's diff surfaces exactly what Anthropic quietly adjusted in their defaults — a form of interpretability-through-transparency that no other lab offers. The thread had 237 points. That Anthropic makes these changes visible at all is itself notable.

Agents at the Infrastructure Layer

Two HN posts this week signal how fast the "agents as first-class internet citizens" shift is materializing. Browser-Use, a browser automation framework, published a post about "reverse CAPTCHAs" — verification systems that keep humans out and let agents in. The standard CAPTCHA is designed to distinguish humans from bots; the reverse CAPTCHA is for agent-native signup flows where you want to confirm the other party is actually an automated system, not a human pretending to be one. The tooling for an agent-native web is being built in real time.

On the inference efficiency frontier: a post about FP4 — 4-bit floating point — explored why extremely low-precision numbers are useful for AI inference. FP4 represents a further compression of the weight representations that models run on, potentially enabling much faster and cheaper inference with acceptable quality tradeoffs. The post broke down the numerical properties and where FP4 is already showing up in production systems. Separately, a Show HN demonstrated Gemma 4's 3.1GB variant running entirely in Chrome via WebGPU, with a prompt-to-Excalidraw diagram demo — no API key, no cloud, local inference at browser speed. The gap between "edge AI" and "cloud AI" in capability terms is narrowing faster than most expected.

Finally: TRELLIS.2, an image-to-3D model, now runs on Apple Silicon without an Nvidia GPU — a Show HN that hit 75 points. A year ago, real-time 3D generation from images required datacenter hardware. The fact that it now runs on consumer Apple chips isn't just a demo win; it's a data point about how quickly frontier AI capabilities are moving from cloud to local.

What This All Means

Takeaways

  1. The jobs conversation is about the wrong thing. The question isn't which tasks AI can automate. It's whether the coordination structures built to manage human-to-human handoffs still make sense when agents can share full context in seconds. Most organizations haven't started reckoning with this.
  2. Demis Hassabis's three missing pieces for AGI are consistency, online learning, and world models. The good news is these are engineering problems with plausible paths. The bad news is they're not solved — and the gap between "impressive in some dimensions" and "reliable across all dimensions" may be larger than benchmarks suggest.
  3. The hardware bottleneck is geopolitical now, not just technical. RAM shortage + bromine dependence on Middle East supply chains means AI's infrastructure ambitions can be disrupted by events that have nothing to do with model research. This deserves more attention from anyone building AI-dependent products at scale.
  4. Security hygiene is collapsing under the pace of AI integration. Three significant security stories in one day (Vercel breach, Notion email leak, Claude Desktop native bridge) suggests that the surface area is expanding faster than the security discipline to match it.
  5. The agent-native internet is being plumbed now. Reverse CAPTCHAs, local inference on consumer hardware, edge models running in the browser — the infrastructure for a world where agents are first-class internet participants is being assembled in real time, mostly in public GitHub repos and Show HN posts.
