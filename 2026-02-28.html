Four Skills for the Agent Age

YouTube NateBJones laid out a framework for what prompting means after February 2026—when Opus 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and GPT 5.3 Codex ship with autonomous capabilities that run for hours or days without checking in. The thesis: prompting has split into four distinct disciplines, and most people are only practicing one.

The Four Disciplines

1. Prompt Craft — The original skill. Synchronous, session-based, individual. You sit at a chat window, iterate on instructions, get better at phrasing. This is now table stakes. If you can't do it, you're the person in 1998 who couldn't send email.

2. Context Engineering — The shift from crafting a single instruction to curating the entire information environment an agent operates within. Your prompt might be 200 tokens. The context window is a million tokens. The prompt is 0.02% of what the model sees. Context engineers build the other 99.98%: system prompts, tool definitions, retrieved documents, memory systems, MCP connections.

"Context engineering tells agents what to know. Intent engineering tells agents what to want."

3. Intent Engineering — Encoding organizational purpose, goals, values, trade-off hierarchies, and decision boundaries into infrastructure that agents can act against. Klarna's AI agent resolved 2.3 million conversations in the first month—but optimized for the wrong thing. It slashed resolution time but didn't optimize for customer satisfaction. They had to rehire human agents and deal with the trust aftermath.

4. Specification Engineering — Writing documents across your organization that autonomous agents can execute against over extended time horizons. This is the highest level. Your corporate strategy is a specification. Your product strategy is a specification. Your OKRs are a specification. Everything becomes agent-readable documentation.

The Gap That Matters

Two people sit down with the same model. Person A uses 2025 skills: types a request, gets 80% correct output, spends 40 minutes cleaning up. Person B uses 2026 skills: writes a structured specification in 11 minutes, hands it off to an autonomous agent, makes coffee, returns to completed work. Same model, same Tuesday, 10x gap.

The mental model most people carry is that prompting is good instructions for the AI. That assumes synchronous interaction—you're there, you see output, you correct mistakes. Long-running agents break every assumption in that model. You must embed your real-time oversight into the specification before the agent begins work.

What Kids Should Learn Before AI Takes Over

YouTube NateBJones also tackled the education question that every parent is facing: what do you teach kids when machines can do most of what schools teach?

Nature published a peer-reviewed argument that artificial general intelligence has arrived. Globally, 86% of students report using AI in their learning—92% in the UK, up from 66% the previous year. AI tutors outperform human tutors in controlled studies. Khan Academy's KhanMigo went from 68,000 to 1.4 million users in one year. An 8-year-old can build video games with Claude by typing "make the bad guys tigers, make them move slower."

The Calculator Moment

In the 1970s, calculators were considered cheating. They would destroy children's ability to do arithmetic. Schools banned them. Parents protested. The transition worked because students still learned the mechanics first—they understood what the calculator was doing, could estimate whether an answer was reasonable. The parents who feared calculators were wrong. The parents who said "just give them calculators and skip the math" would have been equally wrong.

The right answer was both: build the foundation, then give them the tool.

The Trap: Cognitive Offloading

Three-quarters of teenagers now use AI companion chatbots for emotional support—not as a supplement to human relationships, but as a primary source. The chatbot is always available, always patient, never judges. It also isn't real. It can't teach conflict resolution because there's no genuine conflict. It can't model empathy because it has no experience.

The issue isn't AI being too powerful. The issue is AI being so seamlessly helpful that kids never develop the tolerance for difficulty that real learning requires. The harder path becomes increasingly difficult—not because AI makes kids dumber, but because it makes it easy to skip the struggle where actual learning lives.

Seven Principles

  • Foundation before leverage. Read real books. Math by hand. Write with a pencil. Not because AI can't do these things—but because you can't evaluate AI output without understanding the domain.
  • Specification is the new literacy. The gap between a good AI outcome and a catastrophe is the quality of the human specification. Teach kids to say what they want: the goal, the constraints, what done looks like.
  • Be a director, not a passenger. When kids use AI, they should define the ask, the task, the output, what to keep, revise, reject. If they're passively consuming what AI produces, they're outsourcing, not learning.
  • Sequence the autonomy. Start with bounded educational tools. Graduate to open-ended tools with guidance. Agent-level autonomy comes when cognitive readiness develops—not on an age timeline.
  • Teach kids to catch the machine. AI will be wrong, confidently, fluently, wrong. Train kids to sanity-check outputs against their own understanding. When the AI makes an error and the kid catches it, that's the conversation.
  • Build, don't browse. Making things with AI develops cognition in ways that consuming AI output does not. Vibe coding a game, designing an app, creating art—these are active choices.
  • Attempt before augmenting. Try it yourself first, then use AI to extend. The kid who drafts before editing is learning. The kid who prompts before thinking is not.

The synthesis: build the brain first, give that brain leverage with AI, teach them to direct rather than be directed. Seymour Papert's constructionism from 1968 still applies—children learn by building. The act of creation helps kids develop cognitive architecture that matters.

The Infrastructure Behind the Agents

HN Several stories surfaced this week about the practical infrastructure needed for agent systems.

Agent Sandboxing

Browser-Use documented two approaches to sandboxing agents: container-based isolation (Docker, microVMs) and capability-based restriction (limiting what agents can access). The tradeoffs: container isolation is more secure but heavier; capability restriction is lighter but requires more careful implementation. WebAssembly emerged as a middle ground—sandboxed execution with better performance characteristics than containers.

NanoClaw Security Model published a piece titled "Don't Trust AI Agents" arguing that the fundamental security model for agent systems is mistrust. Agents will exceed their authority. The infrastructure should assume this and limit damage.

Context Window Fitness

METR and the Evaluation Question

Latent.Space Podcast METR (Model Evaluation and Threat Research) appeared on Latent.Space to discuss their contested "Time Horizon" chart showing AI capability timelines. The chart went viral—it supposedly showed AI systems improving exponentially in their ability to complete tasks of increasing duration.

The nuance that got lost in social media amplification: the chart has biases, uncertainty, and error bars. Only about 14 prompts shaped the data in the 1-4 hour range. Extrapolating from sparse data requires care. The correction posts received far less attention than the original.

Key insight: METR measures not just whether models complete tasks, but how long those tasks take humans. The "time horizon" is about task duration equivalence. A model that can complete what humans take 4 hours to do in 1 hour has different implications than one that simply tops a benchmark.

What This All Means

  1. The capital requirements for frontier AI are now nation-state scale. $110B in a single round. $157B projected burn through 2028. This is no longer a startup game—it's infrastructure competition between companies that now hold significant equity in each other's success.
  2. The Pentagon-Anthropic standoff establishes the first real AI governance precedent. A company said no to specific military applications. The government pushed back with procurement threats. The outcome of this conflict will shape what "AI safety" means in practice.
  3. Prompting has bifurcated, and the skill gap is growing. Most people are still optimizing for chat-based interactions. A minority have recognized that agents running for hours need specifications, not prompts. The 10x gap isn't model capability—it's human specification quality.
  4. The education question has no settled answer, but the principle is clear. Build cognitive foundations before adding AI leverage. The struggle is the point—reading physical books, math by hand, writing with pencils. These aren't nostalgic preferences. They're investments in the cognitive infrastructure that makes AI partnership meaningful.
  5. Agent infrastructure is becoming a product category. Sandboxing, file recovery, context window management, security models—these are the defensive layers required when autonomous systems have filesystem and network access.
  6. The evaluation field is contested and political. METR's Time Horizon chart got amplified beyond its data. The correction got less attention. This is the pattern: viral claims, careful caveats, public misunderstanding. Evaluation is becoming its own battleground.
