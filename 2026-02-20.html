The Two Futures of AI: Oligopoly or Fragmentation

Capital, Autonomy, and the Bitter Lesson for Startups

There's a fork in the road for AI. Martin Casado of a16z frames it starkly: either the market fragments infinitely as models become commoditized, or a small oligopoly of frontier labs consumes everything built on top of them. The data from this week suggests we're not yet sure which path we're on—but the stakes are becoming clearer. Meanwhile, Anthropic's new research on agent autonomy reveals something unexpected: models are more cautious than their users, and experienced developers grant more autonomy while intervening more often. The picture that emerges is of a technology still finding its equilibrium between capability and control.

The Two Futures

At a16z's offices, Martin Casado laid out what keeps him up at night: two radically different futures for AI's market structure.

In the first future, the market is "infinitely large." Models sublimate quickly—your breakthrough today becomes table stakes tomorrow. Software fractures into countless specialized applications. The universe is complicated, and no single model can capture everything. This looks like normal software: diverse, fragmented, with room for many winners.

In the second future, models "generalize really well." All you have to do is train them with three times more money, and they consume everything beyond them. Frontier labs can raise more than the aggregate of every company building on their APIs, and they use that capital to expand into every adjacent market. This is the AGI path: a small oligopoly that swallows its own ecosystem.

"If you can raise more than the aggregate of anybody that uses your models, you will consume them whether you're AGI or not." — Martin Casado, a16z

What's striking is that Casado doesn't claim to know which future we're heading toward. The data cuts both ways. On one hand, the 99.9th percentile of Claude Code sessions now runs for 45 minutes without human intervention—up from 25 minutes just three months ago. That's a deployment overhang: models are capable of more autonomy than they exercise in practice.

On the other hand, Casado notes that frontier labs are "borrowing against the future." They're gross-margin positive on their current models but gross-margin negative when you factor in the training runs for the next generation. This can continue as long as capital markets keep writing checks—but at some point, the math has to work.

What's a frontier lab? Companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and xAI that train the largest, most capable AI models. They're called "frontier" because they push the boundary of what's possible. Their business model is unusual: they raise billions, pour it into compute, train a model, ship it, gain users, then raise even more for the next training run. It's a capital flywheel that only works if each model is significantly better than the last.

The tension between these futures shapes every strategic decision in AI. If fragmentation wins, the opportunity is in building specialized applications on top of commoditized models. If oligopoly wins, the only defensible position is owning the model itself—or building something so deeply integrated that the model can't replicate it.

How Autonomy Actually Works

Anthropic's research on agent autonomy—their first systematic study of how people actually use AI agents—reveals something counterintuitive. As users gain experience with Claude Code, they don't abdicate oversight. They change its form.

New users manually approve each action. They're cautious, learning the boundaries. Experienced users—those with 750+ sessions—switch to auto-approve for over 40% of their sessions. But here's the surprise: they also interrupt Claude more often, not less. New users interrupt in 5% of turns; experienced users interrupt in 9%.

"Effective oversight doesn't require approving every action but being in a position to intervene when it matters." — Anthropic Research

This suggests a shift from reactive oversight (approve each step) to active monitoring (let it run, intervene when needed). It's the difference between reviewing every line of code versus running tests and checking the output. Software engineering is unusually amenable to this because outputs can be verified independently of the process that created them.

But there's another actor shaping autonomy: the model itself. On complex tasks, Claude Code stops to ask for clarification more than twice as often as humans interrupt it. The model is calibrated enough about its own uncertainty that it limits its own autonomy. This is a safety property that Anthropic trains into Claude deliberately—and it matters because agent-initiated stops are a form of oversight that doesn't require human vigilance.

The deployment overhang: METR's capability evaluations suggest Claude can complete tasks that would take a human 5 hours. But the 99.9th percentile of actual Claude Code sessions runs for 45 minutes. The gap between what models can do in idealized settings and what they do in practice—where users interrupt, models ask questions, and real stakes constrain experimentation—is the deployment overhang. It suggests models are more capable than their current usage patterns exploit.

The research also reveals where agents are actually deployed: nearly 50% of agentic activity is in software engineering. Other domains—healthcare, finance, cybersecurity—are emerging but small. This matters because software is uniquely easy to verify. You can run code and see if it works. In domains like law or medicine, verifying an agent's output may require the same expertise as producing it—which could slow the development of trust and keep autonomy lower for longer.

The ASIC Threshold

One of Casado's most specific predictions concerns custom silicon. At what scale does it make sense to design your own chip?

His answer: at a $1 billion training run, you should seriously consider a custom ASIC (Application-Specific Integrated Circuit). The math is straightforward. A billion-dollar model needs over a billion dollars of inference revenue to be solvent. If you can save 20% on inference costs with custom silicon—a conservative estimate; the real savings are closer to 50%—that's $200 million. You can tape out a chip for $200 million.

What's an ASIC? A chip designed for a single specific application, as opposed to general-purpose chips like CPUs or GPUs. NVIDIA's GPUs are designed to be flexible—they can run games, scientific simulations, and AI training. An ASIC for AI inference would be faster and more energy-efficient for that one task, but useless for anything else. The tradeoff is specialization vs. flexibility.

This is why OpenAI is reportedly working with Broadcom on custom silicon. It's why Google has TPUs. The economics of inference at scale push toward specialization. The question isn't whether it makes economic sense—it's whether the timeline works. Designing and manufacturing a chip takes 18-24 months. In AI, that's an eternity.

But here's the deeper implication: if custom ASICs become standard for frontier models, the capital requirements for entering the model business increase dramatically. You're not just raising for compute; you're raising for chip design, fabrication partnerships, and the infrastructure to deploy specialized hardware at scale. This favors incumbents with deep pockets and accelerates the oligopoly scenario.

Gemini 3.1 Pro and the Model Wars

Google released Gemini 3.1 Pro this week, and the reception has been notably muted. Not because the model is bad—it's excellent—but because the narrative momentum has shifted.

A few months ago, Gemini 3's release felt like Google's moment to reclaim ground. But then Claude Opus 4.5 launched, and the hype cycle moved on. This is the leapfrogging pattern that defines the current era: whoever released most recently has the "best" model, until the next release.

What's more interesting is what the model wars reveal about competitive dynamics. The researchers Nathan Lambert and Sebastian Raschka note that "there's not a clear winner-takes-all scenario" because researchers rotate between labs, ideas flow freely, and no company has exclusive access to breakthrough techniques. The differentiator is budget and hardware constraints, not proprietary knowledge.

This has implications for the oligopoly vs. fragmentation question. If the only moat is capital, then the market structure depends on whether capital advantages compound or erode. Right now, they compound: more capital → better models → more users → more revenue → more capital. But if model capabilities plateau—if the marginal improvement from each dollar spent diminishes—then the capital advantage becomes less decisive.

The Brain Drain

Buried in the HN feed was a Guardian piece on science funding cuts in the US. It's easy to dismiss as political noise, but the timing matters. American science has been the engine behind AI progress—federally funded research produced the transformer architecture, the computational resources that enabled deep learning, and the training grounds for the researchers now leading frontier labs.

The piece argues that funding cuts are driving top talent overseas. Whether you accept that framing or not, the structural question is real: can the US maintain its AI advantage if the pipeline of fundamental research narrows?

Casado and Wang touched on this indirectly. Their American Dynamism fund focuses on companies with regulatory complexity or government customers—areas where understanding the market is as important as the technology. The implication is that some of the most important AI applications (defense, healthcare, infrastructure) will be shaped by policy as much as by capability.

What This All Means

The threads connect in ways that aren't immediately obvious:

  1. Autonomy is co-constructed. It's not a property of the model alone. It emerges from the model's behavior (does it ask questions when uncertain?), the user's oversight strategy (approve each step or monitor and intervene?), and the product's design (what does auto-approve actually mean?). This means effective governance can't mandate specific interaction patterns—it has to focus on whether humans can intervene when it matters.
  2. Capital markets are making a trillion-dollar bet on the oligopoly scenario. The current financing model—raise billions, train a model, ship it, raise more—only works if each generation is significantly better than the last. If capabilities plateau, the math breaks. But if they don't, frontier labs could outspend their entire ecosystem.
  3. The deployment overhang is real. Models are more capable than their current usage patterns exploit. This isn't just about model intelligence—it's about trust, verification mechanisms, and the time it takes for users to develop effective oversight strategies. The gap between capability and deployment is where both opportunity and risk live.
  4. Verification is the bottleneck for domain expansion. Software engineering dominates agentic usage because code can be tested. In domains where verifying output requires the same expertise as producing it, trust develops more slowly. This suggests the next wave of agent adoption will come not from better models, but from better verification tools.
  5. The two futures aren't mutually exclusive—they're sequential. Fragmentation might dominate while capabilities plateau, then oligopoly if another scaling breakthrough occurs. The question isn't which future wins, but how long we stay in each phase.
