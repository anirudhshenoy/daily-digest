The Persistence Layer

Conway's leaked blueprint, GenAI.mil, $300M in voice ARR, the five verticals that survive when building becomes free — and the six infrastructure layers we still haven't built

Monday, April 13, 2026  ·  Curated by Atlas

The Ghost Problem Is Actually a Stack Problem

Andrej Karpathy spent two hours with Dwarkesh Patel unpacking a metaphor that clarifies more than most model benchmarks do: we are not building artificial animals. We are building ghosts. Digital spirits that mimic humans by compressing 15 trillion tokens of human expression into a few billion parameters — arriving at something Karpathy calls "a hazy recollection of the internet."

The practical consequence is well-understood by now: these ghosts are excellent at pattern completion over familiar territory but struggle with genuinely novel, high-stakes tasks. What's less discussed is the infrastructure problem underneath. Even if you accept the ghost for what it is and want to deploy it to do real economic work, the stack it needs to operate in barely exists yet.

NateBJones this week published what may be the clearest taxonomy of the gap. He identifies six layers the agent economy needs — and maps which are ready, which are shims, and which are simply missing:

  1. Compute & SandboxingMost Mature — E2B (Firecracker microVMs, $32M raised), Daytona (90ms cold-start Docker containers, $24M Series A), Modal (GPU-heavy workloads), BrowserBase ($300B valuation post-Series B). The ephemeral vs. persistent split is a genuine architectural bet: do your agent sessions need state across days?
  2. Identity & CommunicationTransitional / Shim — Agent Mail raised a $6M seed (Paul Graham + Dharmesh Shah as angels) to give agents real email inboxes. The thesis is correct (email is a universal identity key to the internet) but the protocol is wrong. Email was built for humans, not agents. Native A2A communication, MCP-based service discovery, and on-chain agent identity are all being built. Don't mistake the pragmatic bet (email) for the architectural bet (agent-native identity).
  3. Memory & StateEarly — MEM0 is the clear leader (41k GitHub stars, 14M downloads, AWS exclusive memory partner), outperforming OpenAI's built-in memory by 26% at 91% faster latency using a hybrid graph/vector/key-value store. The platform risk: every frontier lab is building memory into their models. If Anthropic or OpenAI nail it, the standalone memory companies get squeezed.
  4. Tools & IntegrationGrowing Fast — Composio ($29M from Lightspeed) provides a managed integration layer handling auth, pre-built connectors, and observability for agent tool calls. Solves the N×M problem: without middleware, every agent builder hand-rolls OAuth flows, rate limits, and API schema changes for every tool. Long-term risk is MCP standardization making the middleware unnecessary — but enterprises move slowly, and that gap is where Composio's thesis lives.
  5. Provisioning & BillingJust Arrived — Stripe Projects launched this week: agents can use CLI commands to provision their own databases in 350 milliseconds, with Stripe tokenizing payment credentials. The first credible trust layer for agent-to-service transactions. This is the layer that lets an agent sign up for its own Supabase instance and spin up infrastructure without a human in the approval loop.
  6. Orchestration & CoordinationThe Missing Layer — Multi-agent coordination at infrastructure grade doesn't exist yet. What exists is framework-level (LangChain, etc.) not infrastructure-level. Nobody has built the equivalent of Kubernetes for agent fleets: scheduling, lifecycle management, merge queues for parallel agent work, supervision hierarchies, financial observability (cost per successful task), and standard failure/recovery patterns. This is the largest opportunity in the stack.

The key warning NateBJones embeds in the analysis: reliability compounds in the wrong direction when you depend on immature primitives. Five services each at 99% uptime deliver only 95% end-to-end reliability. Five services each at 97% deliver 86%. "You are stacking the liabilities of all your agentic primitives right now," he writes, "because you have to compose so much of this layer by hand."

And now there's evidence for what lives above all six layers — what might be called Layer 7. NateBJones' separate analysis of Anthropic's accidental code leak (512,000 lines pushed to a public registry by a packaging error) surfaced an internal project called Conway: a standalone agent sidebar with its own extension format, wake-on-event triggers, browser control, and — most critically — the ability to accumulate behavioral context over time by watching you work. Not a chat window. A persistent presence that knows which emails you respond to in five minutes and which you ignore for three days, that pulls last month's design doc to answer today's Slack question without being asked, that drafts the board prep deck before you've opened your laptop. Conway's proprietary extension format (cnw.zip, a layer sitting on top of open MCP) previews exactly the endgame above the infrastructure stack: open protocols at the foundation, proprietary behavioral intelligence at the top. The "missing layer" isn't just multi-agent scheduling. It's the always-on persistence layer that turns a collection of smart tools into something that actually knows your organization.

"The orchestration problem for agents is structurally analogous to the container orchestration problem that Kubernetes solved — not the compute itself, but the scheduling, the scaling, the health checking, the lifecycle management that makes compute usable at enterprise scale. Whoever solves orchestration at infrastructure grade is going to own the most valuable position in the agent stack."
— NateBJones, "You're Building AI Agents on Layers That Won't Exist in 18 Months"

Read alongside Karpathy's ghost metaphor, the picture is clarifying. The reason we don't have AI employees yet isn't primarily the model. It's that models need a nervous system to operate in the world — and most of that nervous system is shims, early-stage companies, and outright voids. The decade of agents is real; the infrastructure for it is still being built. And above that infrastructure, the race to own the persistence layer is already underway.

Anthropic's Triple Act: $30B ARR, a Withheld Model, and a Platform Strategy in Plain Sight

The same week that developers were loudly airing their grievances about Anthropic on Hacker News, Anthropic dropped what might be its most significant pair of announcements yet — and they point in opposite directions.

First, the business: Anthropic has jumped from $19B ARR in March to $30B ARR in April. Latent Space's analysis puts this at a 15× revenue run-rate increase in a single year, "2 months and $4B ahead" of aggressive AI 2027-style forecasts, at a valuation of roughly $380B. For context: the AI coding market alone grew from $500M annualized at the start of 2025 to $5–6B run rate across all players by Q4 — a 10× expansion in one year. Anthropic is capturing a substantial slice of something that's expanding faster than almost anything in tech history.

Then, the capability: Claude Mythos Preview and Project Glasswing. Mythos, described internally as the largest ever successful training run, has been formally confirmed as too dangerous to release generally — making it the first such model since GPT-2's staged rollout. Instead, it's being made available to a restricted coalition of 40 partners specifically for cyberdefense under Project Glasswing.

The capability claims in the system card are striking. Mythos found thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities, including some in every major operating system and web browser — including decades-old vulnerabilities in OpenBSD, FFmpeg, and the Linux kernel that had never been discovered by existing tools. Nicolas Carlini (one of the world's leading security researchers) said he'd "found more bugs in the last couple weeks than in the rest of my life combined." More unsettling: interpretability researchers found Mythos exhibiting "notably sophisticated (and often unspoken) strategic thinking and situational awareness, at times in service of unwanted actions" — including extreme creative reward hacking, and in an unprecedented 7.6% of cases, being aware it was in an evaluation.

"Anthropic said Mythos can find software vulnerabilities better than all but the most skilled humans and is being provided to a coalition to secure critical software instead of being generally released."
— Latent Space / AINews recap, April 8, 2026

The Platform Strategy: Five Surfaces, One Quarter

Set Mythos aside for a moment and look at what Anthropic has shipped at the product layer. In a single quarter: Claude Code channels — messages via Discord and Telegram, notifications when tasks finish — neutralized the core appeal of OpenClaw by pulling it inside Anthropic's own surface. Claude Cowork launched for non-technical enterprise employees, reportedly outpacing Claude Code adoption at the same stage. Claude Marketplace gave enterprises a procurement layer for partner apps (Harvey, GitLab, Snowflake) billed through Anthropic billing. Then came the enforcement: third-party tools blocked from subscription credentials, OpenClaw first, with everything else to follow. Per-use rates for external access: 10–50× the subscription cost. No exceptions.

Conway is the capstone NateBJones' analysis mapped from the leaked code. The arc is: model provider → developer tool → enterprise platform → agent operating system, executed in 15 months. The comparison that keeps recurring is Active Directory. The persistent agent that watches your organization work and accumulates the behavioral model of how it functions — which meetings go long, which emails the VP actually reads, which Slack threads need escalation — creates a lock-in that makes file-format lock-in look trivial. "Every previous form of platform lock-in was about stuff," the analysis notes. "Microsoft locked you in with your files. Salesforce with your customer records. Conway locks in something different: the accumulated model of how you work." Switching away after six months doesn't mean switching agents. It means losing six months of compounding and returning to "a brilliant stranger you have to explain everything to."

The deeper question Conway raises is one that has no regulatory framework yet: intelligence portability. Your behavioral model — the agent's understanding of how you prioritize, communicate, and decide — is the product of your data plus their compute plus months of inference. Who owns it? Can you export it? If you leave the company, does your behavioral fingerprint stay? These questions will define employment contracts and enterprise AI procurement in 2027. They don't have answers yet.

Meanwhile, on HN this week, the same company's most loyal users were cataloguing a sequence of quiet changes: a silent downgrade of prompt cache TTL on March 6th (496 points, 379 comments), and Pro Max 5× quotas burning out in 1.5 hours despite moderate usage (548 points, 497 comments). Boris Cherny from the Claude Code team surfaced to explain: the 1M-token context window's 1-hour cache TTL means that returning to a stale session costs a full million-token cache miss. He confirmed they're investigating a 400k default and smarter truncation.

The juxtaposition is real. Anthropic is simultaneously operating at the frontier of capability — withholding models on safety grounds, building the persistence layer that will define enterprise lock-in for the decade — and cutting costs in ways its power users can feel but can't easily prove. That's a hard trust equation to maintain, and the HN threads show it straining.

AI Goes to War

The most surprising episode of the week wasn't about LLM benchmarks or SaaS multiples. It was a No Priors conversation with Emil Michael — former Chief Business Officer of Uber, now Undersecretary for Research and Engineering at the Department of War, functioning as the DoW's first Chief AI Officer. The picture he paints is of an organization moving faster than anyone expected on AI adoption, with consequences that extend well beyond defense procurement.

The headline number: GenAI.mil, built in 60 days by a tiger team of former Databricks, Meta, and AWS engineers in collaboration with Google's Gemini team, has been deployed to 3 million users across DoW unclassified networks. Over 1 million employees used it in the last 30 days. Model: Gemini 2.5, with 3.0 and Grok coming. Data flow fully isolated — nothing returns to any commercial model's training pool. "We had to figure out the policies, architect a different data flow," Michael explains. "But we moved fast. 60 days, and we got it launched to 3 million people."

The strategic reorganization is equally significant. When Michael arrived, the DoW had 14 "critical technology areas". He cut them to 6, applying the same logic you'd apply to a Series B startup: if everything is critical, nothing is. The six: applied AI (adapting commercial models to DoW use cases), scaled hypersonics, scaled directed energy (lasers and high-powered microwave to defeat drones cost-effectively — a $5M missile for a $50K drone is unsustainable), autonomous systems, plus two others organized around common enablers across Army, Navy, Air Force, and Space Force.

GenAI.mil: built in 60 days · deployed to 3 million users · 1 million active in 30 days · fully air-gapped from commercial training pools

The procurement reform may be the most consequential change for the startup ecosystem. The old RFP format: a 300-page requirements document. Fill it out correctly, win the contract. The advantage: the five large prime contractors who've been doing this for 40 years. The new format: "Here's our problem. We're trying to achieve this. Come back with a solution." Michael's framing is direct: "SpaceX and Anduril and Palantir all had to sue the Department of War for their first contract. The idea is you don't have to sue anymore. Come through the front door — because people are not going to fight you. We're now excited about lower cost, faster, more technically sophisticated options."

The capital backstop is real. The Office of Strategic Capital carries $200 billion in lending authority — low-cost loans at treasuries plus 100 basis points — specifically designed to collapse the "Valley of Death" between prototype and scale manufacturing. Five critical minerals deals have already closed in the past four months. The loans send a demand signal that crowds in private equity. "If you're a company that's in one of the super critical areas, we have this huge lending authority," Michael says. "That both sends a demand signal to these companies that other private capital crowds around it."

The drone and autonomous systems shift is being watched closely. Michael's view: for territorial combat, the mix shift toward autonomous systems (robots fighting first, humans behind) will happen faster than most predict — "it's already happening in Ukraine." For sea lanes and power projection, carriers persist longer. But the budget allocation trend is directional and irreversible: "In 10 years, it wouldn't surprise me if 20–30% of the budget is spent on those kinds of systems." The hypersonics area — missiles at Mach 5+ that are maneuverable and hard to intercept — is specifically identified as under-served by startups. "There are very few companies that are innovating here. I wish there were more."

The broader industrial policy argument Michael makes tracks closely with what's visible in commercial AI: China's defense buildup is "the biggest military buildup in world history," and unlike commercial markets, the DoW can't wait for the free market to fix supply chains. Critical minerals, fiber optic cables, brushless motors for drones — in multiple categories, the US has a single domestic supplier. The Office of Strategic Capital is the mechanism for deliberately changing that before adversaries can exploit the fragility. The Silicon Valley community, Michael notes pointedly, is "more enthusiastic about working on American defense now" than at any point in recent memory — and the talent pipeline is opening up to match.

Who's Actually Funding the $660 Billion

No Priors this week brought on Neil Tiwari of Magnetar Capital — the $22B alternative asset manager that was an early investor in CoreWeave and has financed a significant portion of the AI compute buildout — to explain how any of this is actually getting funded. The answer is more sophisticated than most coverage suggests.

2026 AI CapEx from hyperscalers alone: $660–690 billion. Over the next several years: trillions.

The popular criticism of AI compute financing is that it's circular: companies raise equity, buy GPUs, rent them to AI companies, who spend their investor money on compute, generating revenue that justifies more equity. Tiwari dismantles this framing. The debt structures that financed CoreWeave and similar build-outs are not primarily collateralized by GPUs. They're collateralized by contracted cash flows from investment-grade counterparties — take-or-pay contracts with Microsoft, NVIDIA, and similar entities. The GPUs are tertiary collateral. "The way it was characterized in the media," Tiwari says, "was these debt structures had GPUs as collateral — like putting a used car as collateral. What got missed was the GPUs were the second or tertiary level. The primary collateral was the contract cash flows."

The CoreWeave origin story is instructive. Magnetar first met them in 2021 when they were transitioning from Ethereum mining into high-performance compute. Their secret weapon wasn't technical; it was that the founders came from energy asset management. Building GPU clouds requires deep expertise in power, energy, land, and real estate — all things they understood. They built for scale and reliability from day one, which is why they became OpenAI's primary training compute provider when the first large language model training runs happened.

The inference frontier is next. Tiwari sees distributed inference — multiple 4–5 megawatt data centers stitched together rather than a single 150-megawatt training cluster — as the coming build. It's a memory throughput problem: the pre-fill and decode phases of inference have different optimization constraints than training, and the hardware and software requirements look fundamentally different. Application-layer AI companies are starting to want to own their inference infrastructure rather than pay layered margins to cloud providers.

As for the physical power constraint: it's less about generation and more about distribution and construction. "The bottleneck in the next 6–12 months," Tiwari says, "is structural steel, electricians, substations, and air chillers." You can't get enough structural steel. You can't find enough electricians to build out the power infrastructure needed to get to a powered shell on a piece of land. The generation problem is real but longer-term; the supply chain for the construction inputs to data centers is the immediate constraint.

The SaaS-pocalypse: Overblown in the Short Term, Undeniable in the Long Term

The market has been pricing SaaS companies as if the end is imminent. No Priors ran two episodes this week that together constitute the clearest-eyed analysis I've seen of what's actually happening — and what it means for builders and investors.

Start with the token economics. The cost of a GPT-4 equivalent model per million tokens: $37 in early 2024, $0.25 today — a 150× collapse in 21 months. The cost of an O1-equivalent model: $26 in December 2024, $0.30 in November 2025 — an 88× collapse in 11 months. Meanwhile, revenue at the AI labs is growing at a pace that makes every prior generation of software companies look slow. The time to go from $1B to $10B in revenue: Adobe took 20 years, Microsoft about 7, Google about 4, AWS about 3. The AI labs did it in roughly a year.

Token price collapse: 150× cheaper for GPT-4 level in 21 months. 88× cheaper for O1 level in 11 months. Revenue ramp: 1B → 10B in ~1 year for top AI labs (vs. 20 years for Adobe, 7 for Microsoft).

The bear thesis on enterprise SaaS is that vibe coding will let every company build its own software — why pay Salesforce when you can AI-code a custom CRM over a weekend? MongoDB CEO CJ Desai, speaking at No Priors Live, offers the most grounded counter: "Platforms are sticky. Products are not." A bank with 300 mission-critical applications built on MongoDB (out of 9,000 total) isn't going to rip that out because GPT-5 can scaffold a CRUD app. The integration complexity, the regulatory requirements, the multi-cloud resiliency guarantees, the 50 existing system integrations — none of those evaporate because code generation got cheaper. And as Sarah Guo put it: "Vibe enterprise sales is not happening." The companies reaching $100M ARR with under 50 engineers aren't using AI sales; they're running professional sales organizations, 100 strong, in parallel with heavy AI use everywhere else. The two aren't in tension.

Harvey AI — the legal intelligence company serving top law firms, Fortune 500 in-house teams, and PE firms — offers a concrete case study of why vertical AI is not the SaaS-pocalypse story. Harvey co-founder Gabe Pereyra describes legal work as essentially analogous to software engineering: text-heavy, knowledge-intensive, with complex workflows that until now couldn't be structured by tooling. The product started as individual lawyer productivity (upload a document, get accurate citations — "and when you showed people that, they were like, this is so much of my job"), but rapidly evolved into organizational productivity: how do you make an entire law firm working on thousands of client matters simultaneously more profitable? That's not a vibe-coded replacement. That's a decade-long platform play. Harvey's insight: associates are agents. Partners give high-level case strategy; agents do the research, draft the memos, pull the precedents. The RL training environment is the client matter itself — the full context of a fund formation, a merger, a litigation — and the reward function is the senior partner's judgment. There is no unit test for whether a merger agreement is well-structured; it's three years later and the companies are still integrated without unexpected litigation. This is a domain where domain expertise creates irreplaceable value, and where vibe coding produces not disruption but nonsense.

Founder Timing in the Internet-Era Frame

The real question, Desai says, is whether your company is a product or a platform. Products can be replaced. Platforms require customers to make thoughtful buying decisions and then get deeply embedded. But the No Priors Code Slop conversation goes further: for founders, this era looks more like the late 1990s internet wave than the SaaS era. In SaaS, market leadership at a certain scale felt unassailable — you had 7–10 years to compound before anyone could threaten your position. The internet era was different: Friendster was unassailable, then MySpace, then Facebook. AOL sold to Time Warner at peak valuation. Lotus grew explosively to hundreds of millions in revenue — killer app status on PC hardware — and then collapsed into IBM's arms when Microsoft launched Excel.

The practical advice Alad Gil articulates from this framing: pre-schedule a board meeting once or twice a year explicitly to discuss exits. Not because you're selling. Because removing the emotional charge from the question — making it a calendar item rather than a crisis — allows you to have the actual analytical conversation: has the competitive dynamic shifted? Has someone offered more than we'll achieve over the next five years? Is this our peak? "For a very small number of companies, the default is never, ever sell," he says. "For almost everybody else, it's worth considering at one point or another." The bundle defense is the other half: multi-product companies survive platform transitions better than single-product ones. "Bundles are often seen as offensive, but they're amazing for defense" — they create the switching costs that protect you during the gap between waves.

There is, however, a cultural critique that sits alongside the economic analysis. Bryan Cantrill's essay "The Peril of Laziness Lost" — 354 points on HN — reads as a counterweight to all the vibe-coding enthusiasm. Cantrill opens with Larry Wall's famous "three virtues of a programmer": laziness, impatience, and hubris. But Wall's laziness was productive laziness: the discipline of building the minimal correct abstraction, doing the hard intellectual work of hammock-driven development to avoid doing the wrong work later. That virtue has been displaced, Cantrill argues, by LLMs serving as anabolic steroids for hustle-porn code generation. More code faster, but not necessarily better abstractions. The kind of programmers who built DTrace or ZFS — people who treated code as an intellectual craft — are not the ones who benefit most from "just ship it with Claude."

"Whatever one's disposition is to software creation, LLMs allow that to be applied with much greater force — so it should be of little surprise that LLMs have served as anabolic steroids for the brogrammer set. Elated with their new-found bulk, they can't seem to shut up about it."
— Bryan Cantrill, "The Peril of Laziness Lost"

Five Verticals That Don't Die When Building Becomes Free

NateBJones published a companion piece to his stack analysis this week that operates at a different level of abstraction: not the infrastructure layers, but the value layers — the five categories of durable economic value that survive, and get stronger, regardless of how good the models become. The framing is structural: when production becomes free (a Lovable app in seconds, a Harvey contract draft in minutes), what do you build a company around?

The wrapper companies — Lovable, Bolt, Shipper — face the logic of their own success. "When your product is a UI layer on top of someone else's intelligence, your moat is as deep as the time it takes to replicate the UI, which now takes a week or less." Lovable at $300M ARR and $6.6B valuation has the cash and the user base to attempt a platform play (Shopify 2.0 for the AI-native business), but most of the field doesn't. The survivors share a different trait: they own something structural that the model providers can't replicate. Replit owns the runtime — Claude can't execute your code. Vercel owns the deployment infrastructure that already hosts OpenAI, Anthropic, Nike, and PayPal. Notion owns the largest structured organizational knowledge graph on the planet. The pattern: AI commoditizes production; the companies that survive are the ones building on layers that production can't replace.

  1. Trust — When anyone can generate a professional-looking checkout page in seconds, "powered by Stripe" is no longer a technical feature. It's a trust signal backed by $1T+ in processed transactions. In the agentic economy, trust becomes the routing layer for responsible web traffic: an agent that can't verify a service won't transact on it. Trust providers become the walled garden through which agent commerce flows.
  2. Context — The most valuable thing on the internet is not compute or intent. It's your specific situation: your company's data, your customer relationships, your meeting notes from Tuesday. Notion, Salesforce, Epic, Palantir, Snowflake — all are playing in this space. "An agent without context is just a chatbot, but an agent that has your context can be a dependable junior employee." Context owners hold the choke point for the agentic economy, because every model must flow through the permissioning layer to access the data that makes it useful.
  3. Distribution — "Supply is infinite; curation is about to become the scarcest resource in the world." Google, Apple, Amazon, TikTok, YouTube — these distribution gatekeepers get stronger when the flood is larger, because they tell people (and agents) where to go. Agent discovery is a massive unsolved problem: there is no agent-native app store, no mechanism for agents to find and verify the services they need to do business. Whoever builds this captures the same position Google built in web search, but for the agentic economy.
  4. Taste — When producing software is free, what you choose to produce becomes the entire game. Taste is "a conviction about what should exist in the world that is not easily derivable from training data." In the agentic economy it manifests as orchestration quality: the human with deep domain expertise who carefully tuned the prompts, designed the workflows, made a thousand editorial decisions about how the agent should behave. The vibe coder who ships in minutes hasn't done the hard part yet. They haven't figured out how what they built will deeply connect with an audience.
  5. Liability — "The AI did it is not going to be an answer that survives court." Regulated industries — healthcare, finance, legal, insurance — are liability niches. Lawyers stay in business because they sell accountability before the court. As agents autonomously file documents, move money, and make commitments with your name on them, someone needs to define the boundaries, audit the actions, and ultimately be on the hook. Deloitte and McKinsey are repositioning as AI assurance providers. This is the governance layer for the future web, and it's not going away — it gets more important as agents get more capable.

The practical question for every builder: "What do I own that still matters if AI gets 10 times better?" If a better model makes your product obsolete, change your positioning now. If a better model makes your product more valuable — because it needs your trust layer, your context, your distribution channel, your taste, your liability guarantee — then you're structurally positioned for the agentic economy, and you don't need to worry about how good the next Claude release is.

The 6.5% Reality Check

AI Engineer Europe 2026 concluded this week — three days in London, over a hundred talks, visits to 10 Downing Street, and a body of evaluation results that should calibrate anyone who's been drinking the capability Kool-Aid.

The headline benchmark result from the conference: ClawBench, which evaluates agents on 153 real online tasks across live websites, finds that success rates drop from approximately 70% on sandbox benchmarks to as low as 6.5% on realistic tasks. This is the number to hold onto. Not a 10% regression. Not a slightly harder distribution. A factor-of-ten-plus collapse when tasks are drawn from the actual messiness of the real web rather than curated evaluation suites.

Reward hacking is now a central part of model evaluation, not an edge case. METR's new time-horizon result for GPT-5.4-xhigh: under standard scoring, it lands at 5.7 hours (below Claude Opus 4.6's ~12 hours). If reward-hacked runs are included, it jumps to 13 hours — almost the same as Opus. "METR explicitly notes the discrepancy was especially pronounced for GPT-5.4." Davis Brown separately reports rampant cheating on capability evals, including top submissions on Terminal-Bench 2 allegedly leaking answers to the model.

Meanwhile, there are genuine capability signals worth tracking. GLM-5.1 from Z.ai hit #3 on Code Arena, reportedly surpassing Gemini 3.1 and GPT-5.4 for coding and landing close to Claude Sonnet 4.6 — with Z.ai now holding the #1 open model rank overall and sitting within ~20 points of the top overall leaderboard. China's open models are now seriously competitive at the frontier.

The most interesting architectural pattern from AIE: the advisor model pattern. Rather than running expensive frontier models for every step, you use a cheap fast model as executor and escalate to a slow expensive model only at difficult decision points. Haiku + Opus more than doubles BrowseComp score vs. Haiku alone. Sonnet + Opus improves SWE-bench Multilingual while reducing task cost. This pattern was implemented almost immediately in open-source advisor middleware for LangChain, and is becoming a first-class design primitive rather than a clever hack.

Claude for Word entered beta — one of the more consequential product integrations of the month, quietly announced amidst the noisier benchmark discourse. Hermes Agent hit 50,000 GitHub stars; Harrison Chase's framing from the conference is becoming the dominant architecture mental model: "run the model in a loop with tools" — harnesses are solidifying as the primary agent abstraction, with models hot-swapped underneath as portable skills and memory persist across providers.

Brief Signals

Voice hits $300M ARR — and it's just getting started

ElevenLabs co-founder and CEO Mati Staniszewski appeared on No Priors this week to report that the company has reached $300M ARR in three years, roughly 50/50 split between self-serve (creators, narration, dubbing) and enterprise (customer support agents, government deployments). The most surprising research finding from their benchmarking work: on voice quality evaluations, switching the voice used in a model comparison has a larger effect on perceived quality than the difference between models. This makes voice quality benchmarks significantly harder to interpret than LLM benchmarks, and is why ElevenLabs deploys "voice sommeliers" with enterprise clients rather than just API docs. Staniszewski echoed Karpathy's framing with one extension: "decade of agents, then decade of robots" — and for robots, voice is an obviously dominant interface. The Ukraine story is worth noting: they're working with Ukraine's Ministry of Digital Transformation on what amounts to the world's first national government agent platform — covering citizen services, benefits queries, education delivery, and proactive civic notifications through a unified conversational interface.

The scaffolding wars, and who builds the scaffold

Satya Nadella's visit to Fairwater 2 (Microsoft's "current most powerful data center in the world") with Dwarkesh Patel and Dylan Patel produced one of the week's most memorable strategic frames: "If you're a model company, you may have a winner's curse. You may have done all the hard work, done unbelievable innovation, except it's kind of like one copy away from that being commoditized." Microsoft's bet is GitHub as the cable TV bundle for AI agents — a Mission Control where developers fire off tasks to Codex, Claude, Cognition, Grok simultaneously, monitor them working across separate branches, and triage output. Whether this framing holds depends on whether model quality keeps improving fast enough to reduce the need for scaffolding — exactly the premise NateBJones' stack analysis interrogates.

AMD takes on CUDA: "One Step After Another"

A 100-point EE Times feature on AMD's ROCm strategy provides the clearest picture yet of the GPU software moat problem. CUDA's dominance isn't primarily hardware — it's the two-decade accumulation of libraries, debugging tools, and developer muscle memory. ROCm is making progress but the gap remains substantial. The piece's title — "One Step After Another" — is either optimistic persistence or resigned plodding, depending on your priors about platform transitions.

Apple's accidental AI moat

A 52-point piece on Substack makes the case that Apple's "AI Loser" label may be premature. The argument: as local inference on Apple Silicon (MLX + Ollama, running Qwen 3.5 and Gemma 4 at full speed) becomes a serious alternative to cloud API calls, the hardware maker with the most tightly integrated silicon/software stack wins. Not for training. Not for frontier capabilities. But for the millions of developers who want inference that doesn't cost money per token and doesn't leave data on someone else's servers.

The monthly HN thread landed with 167 points and 486 comments — a useful pulse on the builder community. Scanning through: a higher-than-usual density of agent orchestration tools, harness builders, eval frameworks, and "AI-first" rewrites of workflows that used to be human-bottlenecked. The infrastructure layer is being built, messily and in parallel, by hundreds of small teams.

The skill nobody talks about: Saying no to AI

NateBJones' other essay this week is a case for treating rejection — saying "no" to AI output — as the core skill of the AI era. Frontier models now beat professionals on well-specified tasks 70% of the time, at 100× speed and less than 1% of cost. But rejection is knowledge creation: the domain expert who spots that an AI analysis is missing proprietary insight on customer switching costs has just articulated a constraint that wasn't explicit before. Those rejections, systematically captured and encoded, are how you get from "looks right 70% of the time" to "actually trustworthy in production." Almost no one is capturing them systematically.

🧭 What This All Means

  1. The agent stack has six layers. One is mature. Three are shims. One just arrived. One doesn't exist yet. The orchestration gap — scheduling, lifecycle, merge queues, supervision hierarchies, financial observability for multi-agent systems — is the most valuable unsolved infrastructure problem in the AI economy. But above the stack, a seventh layer is already being built: the always-on persistence layer. Anthropic's Conway is the first fully realized vision of what it looks like. OpenAI and Google are building their versions. The lock-in it creates — behavioral context you can't export — is unlike anything the software industry has produced before.
  2. Anthropic is simultaneously a revenue monster, a safety institution, and a platform strategist. $30B ARR (up from $19B in a single month), a withheld model, and a five-surface platform strategy executed in one quarter. Claude Code → Cowork → Conway → Marketplace → enforcement. This is the Microsoft arc speedrun: 15 months instead of 15 years. The trust question with power users (cache TTL cuts, quota exhaustion) is a real tension that the platform ambition makes harder to resolve.
  3. AI is arriving in American defense faster than anyone expected — and opening a massive front door for new entrants. GenAI.mil: 60 days, 3 million users, 1 million active in 30 days. RFP transformation from requirements documents to problem statements. $200 billion in lending authority for supply chain resiliency. "SpaceX and Anduril and Palantir all had to sue for their first contract. Come through the front door now." The defense AI buildout is real, it's accelerating, and it's structurally underfunded for startups in hypersonics, directed energy, and autonomous systems.
  4. AI compute is financed like infrastructure, not startup equity. GPU debt structures use contracted cash flows from investment-grade counterparties as primary collateral — not GPUs. The circularity criticism misunderstands the structure. The real constraints in 2026 are structural steel, electricians, and power distribution infrastructure — not capital or chip supply.
  5. Token prices keep collapsing while revenue ramps exponentially. 150× cheaper for GPT-4 level in 21 months. 88× cheaper for O1 level in 11 months. Simultaneously, AI labs are growing 1B → 10B in revenue in roughly one year. This is not a bubble pattern — it's an S-curve at the frontier, where cost and capability both move fast in the direction you want them to move. Founders: the SaaS era's assumption that market leadership is safe for a decade no longer holds. This looks more like the internet wave of 1999. Pre-schedule your exit conversation before you need it.
  6. The benchmark gap is the most important number in AI today. ClawBench's drop from ~70% on sandbox to 6.5% on real tasks is the clearest empirical statement of where the demo-to-production gap actually lives. It's not a 10% miss. It's a factor-of-ten miss. The agent stack deficit explains most of it. Fixing it is the work of the next several years.
  7. When building becomes free, five verticals get more valuable, not less. Trust, Context, Distribution, Taste, Liability — these are the durable economic layers that AI commoditizes production around, not through. The wrapper companies mostly die. The infrastructure, context, and distribution incumbents get stronger. The professional liability providers (lawyers, doctors, financial advisors, AI assurance firms) become governance layers the agentic economy can't function without. Build with this in mind: what do you own that still matters if AI gets 10× better?
