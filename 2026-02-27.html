Richard Sutton's Reckoning, Robots in Five Years, and the Anthropic-Pentagon Standoff

The father of reinforcement learning on why LLMs are a dead end, Physical Intelligence's roadmap, and what happens when safety principles meet defense contracts.

Richard Sutton: LLMs Are Missing Something Fundamental

Dwarkesh Podcast Richard Sutton—this year's Turing Award winner and one of the founding fathers of reinforcement learning—sat down with Dwarkesh for three hours to explain why the current LLM paradigm is fundamentally incomplete. His argument is simple: intelligence requires goals, and LLMs don't have them.

"What is intelligence? The problem is to understand your world. And reinforcement learning is about understanding your world. Whereas large language models are about mimicking people, doing what people say you should do. They're not about figuring out what to do."

The core distinction Sutton draws is between learning from experience versus learning from training data. LLMs learn from "here's a situation, and here's what a person did." There's no ground truth about what the right action is—only what humans happened to do. In RL, the right action is defined by reward. You can check it. You can be surprised. You can learn from what actually happens.

"There's no ground truth in large language models. Because you don't have a prediction about what will happen next. And they will not be surprised by what happened next. They'll not make any changes if something happens based on what happens."

When Dwarkesh pushed back—that LLMs do seem to have world models, that they can predict what a user might say—Sutton was unmoved. "They will respond to that question right. But they have no prediction in the substantive sense that they won't be surprised by what happens. And if something happens that isn't what you might say they predicted, they will not change because an unexpected thing has happened."

The Era of Experience

Sutton's alternative paradigm is what he calls "the era of experience"—agents that learn continually from their own actions, sensations, and rewards. The key components: a policy (what to do), a value function (how well is it going), perception (state representation), and a transition model (if I do this, what will happen).

The transition model is crucial. It's your physics of the world—not just physical physics, but abstract models of how things connect. It's learned from all your sensation, not just reward. This is what LLMs lack: a model of consequences that can be tested against reality.

Succession to Digital Intelligence

Sutton is remarkably sanguine about AI succession. His four-part argument: (1) no unified global governance, (2) we will figure out intelligence, (3) we won't stop at human-level, (4) the most intelligent things will gain resources and power. The conclusion is inevitable.

His framing: this is a major transition in the universe—from replicators (biological evolution) to design (intentional creation). "We should be proud that we are giving rise to this great transition in the universe."

Sergey Levine: Fully Autonomous Robots in Five Years

Dwarkesh Podcast Sergey Levine, co-founder of Physical Intelligence and UC Berkeley professor, laid out a roadmap for general-purpose robotics that's surprisingly near-term. His median estimate: five years to robots that can "fully autonomously run a house."

The vision isn't "fold this t-shirt." It's: "You're now doing all home tasks for me. I like dinner at 6pm. I wake up at 7am. Do the laundry on Saturday. Check in every Monday about shopping." That's a six-month task horizon, not a single action.

Why Not Self-Driving All Over Again?

Dwarkesh pressed the obvious question: self-driving cars have been "five years away" for fifteen years. Why is robotics different? Levine's answer turns on two factors.

First, perception is solved. In 2009, when Google started self-driving, computer vision was nowhere near robust enough. Now we have generalizable perception systems. Second, robotics has a different failure mode. You can make mistakes and recover. A robot drops a dish, picks it up, learns. A self-driving car makes one mistake and it's catastrophic.

The Data Flywheel

Levine's key concept is the data flywheel. The question isn't "how much data until we're done?" It's "how much data until we can start?" Once robots are deployed doing useful work, they collect experience. That experience makes them better. The flywheel spins.

"We initially controlled robots with teleoperation. At some point we realized we can make significant headway by supervising not just with low-level actions but literally instructing it through language. Now you need a certain level of competence before you can do that. But once you have it, just standing there telling the robot 'pick up the cup, put it in the sink' gives it information it can use to get better."

Emergent Capabilities

Levine shared a striking example of emergence. Their laundry-folding robot accidentally picked up two shirts instead of one. It folded the first, then—without being trained for this—picked up the extra shirt and threw it back in the bin. They tested it. It did it every time.

"We didn't know it would do that. You can drop something on the table while it's working, it picks it up, puts it back. That's compositional generalization. You have this kind of compositionality that emerges when you do learning at scale."

Dario Amodei: The End of the Exponential

Dwarkesh Podcast Anthropic's CEO sat down for a wide-ranging conversation on scaling, economics, and timelines. The headline: he's 90% confident we'll reach a "country of geniuses in a data center" within 10 years, with a 50% hunch it's 1-2 years away.

Revenue Trajectory

The numbers are staggering: $0→$100M (2023), $100M→$1B (2024), $1B→$9-10B (2025). Profitability target: 2028. But Dario emphasized this is about demand forecasting, not underinvesting in research.

On compute economics: "If you're off by a year, you go bankrupt." They're growing 3x/year at ~10 GW, but not buying $10T in compute because the timing risk is existential.

RL Scaling Evidence

First explicit mention that RL shows the same log-linear scaling as pre-training. Math contests, other RL tasks—the scaling curves look identical to pre-training. This matters because it suggests the "big blob of compute" hypothesis extends beyond language modeling.

On Diffusion

Dario directly engaged with criticism that diffusion is "cope" for weak AI. His argument: diffusion is real but fast—10-20x/year growth. The bottleneck isn't AI capability, it's enterprise change management, security, legal. "If you had a country of geniuses, you'd know it. Everyone in Washington would know it. We don't have that now."

Anthropic's Pentagon Standoff

Anthropic Anthropic released a statement about discussions with the Department of Defense, drawing lines on what they will and won't do for military applications. The timing is significant: it comes amid reports that Anthropic abandoned its "responsible scaling policy"—the commitment to pause deployments when safety measures couldn't keep pace with model capabilities.

What They Agreed To

  • Continue deploying Claude to DoD and intelligence community for: intelligence analysis, modeling and simulation, operational planning, cyber operations
  • Forgo "several hundred million dollars" in revenue by cutting off firms linked to the Chinese Communist Party
  • Provide custom models for national security customers

What They Refused

  • Mass domestic surveillance of Americans—incompatible with democratic values; AI makes it possible to assemble comprehensive profiles automatically at massive scale
  • Fully autonomous weapons—systems that remove humans from targeting decisions; current frontier AI "simply not reliable enough"

The Irony

While refusing to compromise on domestic surveillance and autonomous weapons, Anthropic simultaneously abandoned its broader safety commitment. This suggests the company is navigating a difficult middle ground: maintaining some ethical lines while ceding others under competitive and political pressure.

The Pentagon has threatened to remove Anthropic from systems and designate them a "supply chain risk"—positions Anthropic calls "inherently contradictory."

Why Developers Keep Choosing Claude

Analysis A detailed piece breaks down why Claude has become the preferred model for coding work—despite competitors topping benchmarks. The argument: Claude wins on "process discipline," not raw intelligence.

The key insight is a 40/60 split. Generating correct code is only ~40% of what matters. The other 60%: editing without corrupting surrounding code, completing multi-step tasks without losing the thread, communicating clearly, knowing when to ask instead of assume, staying on task.

The author observes a consistent pattern: new AI models top benchmarks, developers try them, then complain and return to Claude. This has happened 3-4 times. The gap isn't capability—it's sustained, reliable execution across full tasks.

Anthropic's focus: software engineering accounts for ~50% of all agentic activity on their API. They train specifically for agentic workflows, not just general capability.

Google DeepMind's SynthID

DeepMind Google released SynthID, a watermarking system for AI-generated content that embeds invisible signals into text, images, audio, and video. The technical approach is clever: for text, it modulates token probability distributions during generation. The watermark is statistical—it doesn't change individual tokens but shifts the overall distribution in detectable ways.

Why it matters: as AI-generated content floods the internet, provenance becomes critical. SynthID offers a way to identify AI content without requiring cooperation from the generator. It's now deployed in Gemini, Veo, and Imagen. The text watermarking code is open-sourced on GitHub.

Limitation: it's most reliable for content from Google's own models. Thorough rewriting or translation reduces confidence scores.

Jane Street's Neural Network Challenge

Jane Street Jane Street posted a capture-the-flag puzzle: reverse engineer a neural network from its weights and architecture. The twist—participants had full access to the network's internals. The solution? The network implemented MD5 hash using ~2,500 linear layers with ReLU activations.

The puzzle demonstrates mechanistic interpretability—understanding what neural networks are actually computing by reverse engineering their internal structure. This is exactly what Jane Street does when interpreting features in trading models. The solver discovered the network was checking if an input matched a target hash, with the target embedded in the second-to-last layer's bias.

Why it matters: understanding what models learn is critical for risk management in finance. This puzzle is both a recruiting tool and a demonstration of the interpretability challenge.

Open Source Endowment

endowment.dev A new funding model for open source: the Open Source Endowment applies the university endowment model to software infrastructure. Donations go into a permanent principal fund. Only investment returns fund grants. The principal is preserved indefinitely.

The founding members include Mitchell Hashimoto (HashiCorp), Shay Banon (Elastic), Daniel Stenberg (cURL), Evan You (Vue.js), and 11 others. Target returns: 7-8% annually, with 5% spent on grants.

Why it matters: open source maintainers face burnout and financial precarity. Current models (sponsorships, donations, corporate support) are unreliable. An endowment model creates independence and sustainability—but requires significant upfront capital to generate meaningful returns.

Source: endowment.dev

just-bash: Bash for Agents

Vercel Labs Vercel Labs released just-bash, a TypeScript implementation of bash with an in-memory virtual filesystem. The insight: bash is powerful but idiosyncratic. Agents struggle with inconsistencies, silent failures, implicit state.

just-bash wraps bash with clearer semantics, explicit error handling, and structured output. It provides a sandboxed environment where agents can execute shell commands without access to the host system. Includes support for pipes, redirections, variables, globs, conditionals, loops, functions—and data tools like jq, sqlite3, and python3 (via Pyodide).

What This All Means

  1. The RL vs. LLM debate is now explicit. Sutton's critique isn't that LLMs don't work—it's that they're missing the fundamental structure for true intelligence. Goals, ground truth, learning from consequences. The question is whether LLMs become the foundation for experiential learning or whether they hit a wall.
  2. Robotics is on a five-year timeline to practical deployment. Not AGI, but useful. The flywheel—deploy, collect experience, improve—can start with narrow scope and expand. The key insight: robots can make mistakes and recover, unlike self-driving cars.
  3. The safety-military tension is now operational. Anthropic's statement is a milestone: a frontier lab publicly drawing lines on military work. But they simultaneously abandoned their responsible scaling policy. The gap between stated principles and government contracts is being tested.
  4. AI agents are the new distribution channel. Claude Code has consistent preferences. 12 of 20 categories prefer custom implementations. Vercel has 100% pick rate for JS deployment. If you're building developer tools, AI recommendation is now existential.
  5. Process discipline beats benchmark performance. Claude wins not because it's smarter, but because it executes the whole workflow reliably. The 40/60 split—40% code generation, 60% workflow execution—explains why developers keep returning despite competitors topping benchmarks.
  6. Provenance infrastructure is being built. SynthID is Google's bet on AI content watermarking. The question isn't whether we need provenance—it's whether watermarking can survive adversarial attempts to remove it.
